A Note About Git Branches:

We probably screwed this one up. We have been using local git branches on our personal IDEs to develop new features, test their integration, and then merge back into the master before pushing back to GitHub. Individual developers such as outstanding-mouse had up to 4 different feature branches going at a time to contain propagation of bugs and ensure smooth integration with the team’s master branch. However, it was not until this last sprint that we realized branches are only trackable and accessible across the team if pushed to the origin. We belatedly employed this strategy to manage rework that had to be done without interrupting ongoing database integration efforts, but we clearly did not make the extent of our Git utilization strategy transparent to outsiders. As opposed to our local branch strategy, a much more Agile-embracing approach would have been to create and utilize public (origin/GitHub hosted) branches for feature development, testing/integration, and staging, in addition to our mainline master. Oops.

That being said, our team universally agrees that version control is a mysterious, powerful, and awe-inspiring force that we are not worthy of. Each one of us has an example about when Git saved the day, from allowing us to trash our crappy attempts and pull back down our known working master code to rolling back to previous commits when cautious-coyote accidentally overwrote the entire database connection schema for the app. We <3 Git, and hope that one day, Git will <3 us too.

Traffi findings:

We had 12 sources of traffic. The most common was traffic from SOM (44.15%), followed by yale.edu (30.32%). Every other segment was less than 10% with medicine (9.25%), direct (5.8%), law (5.13%) being the top five. May of the others were us or the TAs looking at the website (see chart below). Our overall conversion rate was .78%, with our group testing being the most generous :). Of the student groups, law students were the most generous with 2.17% donation rate and stingy SOM students were next with .37% rate. The medical student loans and future of many years of residency must have prevented the medical students from donating, with no one contributing.

A/B testing results:

Our experient directed 55 people to donate and 68 people to support. We found donate to have a much higher conversion rate (7.27) than support (1.47%). This was consistent for all days except Dec 11 (see chart below).
